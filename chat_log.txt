It involves an unusually strong and widespread warming of sea surface temperatures (SSTs) across the central and eastern equatorial Pacific Ocean, coupled with significant atmospheric responses.  The formation of a Super El Niño is essentially an intensification of the processes that lead to a regular El Niño, driven by a powerful positive feedback loop between the ocean and atmosphere.  Here's a step-by-step explanation of how it forms:  1.  **Pre-existing Conditions (Often Neutral or La Niña):**     *   Leading up to a Super El Niño, the Pacific may be in a neutral state or even a La Niña (cooler eastern Pacific). During La Niña, strong trade winds push a lot of warm water to the western Pacific, causing the thermocline (the boundary between warm surface water and cold deep water) to deepen there and become shallower in the east. This creates a "recharge" of warm water in the west, acting as potential fuel for a future El Niño.  2.  **Initial Trigger: Weakening of Trade Winds:**     *   The process often begins with a significant weakening of the easterly trade winds in the western and central equatorial Pacific. This weakening can be sporadic, perhaps initiated by atmospheric disturbances like the Madden-Julian Oscillation (MJO), which can generate "westerly wind bursts" (brief periods where winds blow from west to east).  3.  **Eastward Propagation of Warm Water (Kelvin Waves):**     *   When the trade winds weaken, the piled-up warm water in the western Pacific is no longer held back. It begins to slosh eastward in the form of subsurface warm water waves called **equatorial Kelvin waves**. These waves travel along the equator, deepening the thermocline as they move eastward.  4.  **Sea Surface Warming in the East:**     *   As the Kelvin waves reach the eastern Pacific, the deepened thermocline brings warmer water closer to the surface, and eventually, warm water from the west displaces the cooler upwelled water. This leads to a significant increase in sea surface temperatures (SSTs) in the central and eastern equatorial Pacific.  5.  **Atmospheric Response and Bjerknes Feedback Loop (The Core Mechanism):**     *   **Atmospheric Warming:** The warmer SSTs in the central/eastern Pacific warm the overlying atmosphere.     *   **Convection Shift:** This warmer air becomes less dense and rises, leading to increased convection (thunderstorms and rainfall) in the central Pacific, shifting the typical rainfall patterns away from the western Pacific (Indonesia/Australia) and towards the central/eastern Pacific.     *   **Pressure Gradient Change:** This shift in convection alters the atmospheric pressure patterns. The rising air creates an area of lower pressure in the central Pacific, while sinking air over the western Pacific creates higher pressure.     *   **Further Weakening of Trade Winds:** This reduced pressure gradient across the Pacific *further weakens the trade winds*.     *   **Positive Feedback:** This is the critical step: the weakened trade winds allow *even more* warm water to move eastward, leading to *even warmer* SSTs, which *further weakens the trade winds*, and so on. This ocean-atmosphere coupling, known as the **Bjerknes Feedback**, is what amplifies the initial warming.  6.  **Intensification to a "Super" El Niño:**     *   For an El Niño to become "Super," this Bjerknes feedback loop must be exceptionally strong and sustained. This usually involves:         *   **Very Strong and Persistent Westerly Wind Bursts:** Repeated or unusually powerful westerly wind bursts that provide a continuous push of warm water eastward and maintain the weakening of trade winds.         *   **Large Reservoir of Warm Water:** A significant amount of warm water accumulated in the western Pacific prior to the event, providing ample "fuel" for the eastward transport.         *   **Deep and Widespread Thermocline Anomalies:** The eastward-moving Kelvin waves create a very deep and extensive thermocline anomaly across much of the equatorial Pacific, allowing sustained and widespread surface warming.         *   **Strong Coupling:** The ocean and atmosphere must be strongly coupled, meaning changes in one rapidly and effectively reinforce changes in the other.  Essentially, a Super El Niño forms when the natural ocean-atmosphere feedback mechanisms that drive El Niño become exceptionally powerful, leading to a much larger displacement of warm water, a more profound shift in atmospheric circulation, and consequently, more extreme global weather impacts.
